Section 1: Overview

Sit-Stand Desks have become a popular topic over the last decade, spurring conversation and controversy. There is inconsistent research supporting the use of sit-stand workstations. Some studies suggest these desks can improve focus and productivity while others show increased lower extremity pain and fatigue. There are even inconsistent parameters for the specific durations to sit and stand throughout a workday. The most important consideration is that everyone is different and has different work needs. If you use a sit-stand desk or plan to, it's important to temper your daily habits within the range of your abilities. Standing for too long for some can lead to similar musculoskeletal fatigue and breakdown found in the seated worker. When used correctly, sit-stand desks can appropriately increase your work endurance, focus, and decrease the many aches and pain related to being seated for 8-hours a day or more.

Standing is a static position, creating many isometric contractions in various muscles throughout your body. An isometric contraction is stationary activation of the muscles without the allowance of joint movement. Static contractions can quickly lead to general muscular fatigue and can begin to negatively shift posture and positioning. Movement is one of the body's natural mechanisms to shift swelling and supply oxygen to the muscles during continuous use. While the muscles fatigue, isometric contractions can cause the joints of the spine, hips, and knees to become strained. Without stability granted from your muscles, the forces shift to the joints as the last line of defense to contain the effects of gravity. Further, attempting to stand for long hours will more than likely lead to loading weight through your elbows, wrists, or hands. This can quickly and unintentionally create hazardous conditions for those regions of the body like tendonitis, or nerve-related pain through the arms, wrists, and hands.

Alternately, standing for transitional breaks can be helpful to increase blood flow and prevent many forms of musculoskeletal pain, but should be done in moderation. A general recommendation is to stand, if able, for 10-20 minutes every hour as tolerated. This change in position creates the opportunity for the various muscles of the body to stretch, recieve increased circulation, and decrease relevant muscular stiffness, strain, or nerve tension while remaining focused on your work. Many users can stand for much longer with success, but it is important to consider that every job and every worker is different, requiring unique provisions. What is most important is to be personally aware of the fatigue, tension, or strain that develops through your body while you continue to stay engaged in your work. Then you are more able to make responsible decisions based on how your body is tolerating the changing environment. Sit-stand desks are a tool that may facilitate movement and increased blood flow throughout the day but should be used thoughtfully. Sit-stand desks consist of many different properties and features. Consider what aspects are required for your job and body type so that your desk can be matched to you as an individual and your needs will be perfectly met.

Section 2: Brief Equipment Guide

Many workstation variations promote the sit-stand mentality, ranging from fully adjustable desks to table-top workstations. All have their place for different workers. For example, a graphic designer would benefit from a work station which rises in its entirety as opposed to just the computer and keyboard tray. This decreases the need to repetatively reach for items which have not been also raised and allows ample space for drawing, writing, etc. Alternatively, an administrative assistant may only require an adjustable monitor and keyboard if they are primarily dependent on the computer for their work - not requiring additional equipment or surface area. It's also important to know what your body can tolerate. Some desks require a degree of force to manually pull / push / crank to the correct height while others are fully electric. Consider some of the following questions to ask yourself:

Thoughts to consider when selecting your sit-stand desk or workstation:

  • Do you need additional work space apart from a monitor, keyboard, and mouse?
  • What kind of budget do you have available?
  • Does the new workstation allow for keyboard and mouse adjustability when working at different heights?
  • Do you need the whole workstation to lift or just the computer + keyboard / mouse for intermittent breaks?
  • Do you want a fully new desk or something to be added to your current workstation?
  • Will the new desk require additional equipment to maintain adequate adjustability for good ergonomics (Monitor stand, keyboard tray, etc)?
  • What will fit in your current office space?
  • Do you have any current injuries or pains which prevent you from manually adjusting your desk?

These questions should help guide you to select what equipment will work best for you. If you have any further questions regarding sit-stand desks or general ergonomics, feel free to reach out to Root Ergonomics.
